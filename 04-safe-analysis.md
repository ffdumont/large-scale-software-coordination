# 4. SAFe: Analytical Decomposition

## Research question

Which problems does SAFe attempt to solve, and which of its elements are principles, mechanisms, ceremonies, roles, or vocabulary?

## Analytical stance

This section should neither promote nor reject SAFe ideologically. SAFe should be analyzed as a bundle of mechanisms responding to real large-scale coordination problems.

## Key questions

### What SAFe tries to solve

Do not start by describing SAFe. Start by describing the problems:

- alignment across many teams;
- dependency management;
- cross-team planning;
- predictability;
- governance;
- portfolio prioritization;
- synchronization of delivery;
- visibility for leadership;
- coordination across multiple ARTs or work streams.

### Critiques to analyze

For each critique:

1. origin;
2. frequency;
3. underlying cause;
4. evidence level;
5. intrinsic issue or implementation issue?

Candidate critiques:

- bureaucratic overhead;
- too many roles;
- too top-down;
- false agility;
- excessive planning;
- local loss of autonomy;
- vocabulary rejection;
- framework compliance over outcomes.

### Benefits to analyze

Distinguish between:

- intrinsic benefits of the mechanism;
- benefits caused by good execution discipline;
- benefits caused simply by having an explicit operating model.

## Decomposition model

| SAFe element | Principle | Mechanism | Ceremony | Role | Vocabulary | Can it be simplified? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| PI Planning | Alignment, dependency visibility | Periodic multi-team planning | Yes | No | Yes | Likely |
| ART | Stable coordination unit | Team-of-teams boundary | No | No | Yes | Context-dependent |
| Solution Train | Multi-ART coordination | System-level integration and synchronization | No | No | Yes | Only if required by coupling |
